> Researchers at King\'s College London found that the drug Tideglusib
> stimulates the stem cells contained in the pulp of teeth so that they
> generate new dentine \-- the mineralised material under the enamel.
>
> Tideglusib switches off an enzyme called GSK-3 which prevents dentine
> from carrying on forming. Scientists showed it is possible to soak a
> small biodegradable sponge with the drug and insert it into a cavity,
> where it triggers the growth of dentine and repairs the damage within
> six weeks.
>
> Professor Paul Sharpe, lead author of the study, of the Dental
> Institute, from King\'s College London, said: \"The simplicity of our
> approach makes it ideal as a clinical dental product for the natural
> treatment of large cavities, by providing both pulp protection and
> restoring dentine. \"In addition, using a drug that has already been
> tested in clinical trials for Alzheimer\'s disease provides a real
> opportunity to get this dental treatment quickly into clinics.\"
